AR Mummy X-Ray Viewer

Role: App Developer | Learn more here

I worked as part of an interdisciplinary team to provide new experiences to museum visitors by using AR technology. We used CT scan data in order to learn as much as possible about a 2000 year old mummy, and created an iPad app to teach visitors and encourage them to learn more.

I took scan data and experimented with different ways of displaying it. I generated 3D models of the data using a MATLAB library, and then cleaned up the results in Blender. From there, I designed and programmed an AR app using iOS's ARKit and wrote an iOS Metal shader to display the 3D models.

We were originally hoping to have the app align the models with the actual mummy on its own, but after discovering that this functionality was not built into ARKit, this became an obstacle. After discussion with our supervisors and experimentation with other ways of aligning the models, we decided to create a system where museum staff would have a button that would lock the model to the iPad, allowing them to move the device to place the data.

Through this project, I gained experience working with modern AR technologies, and I learned about cooperating with people from a variety of non-coding backgrounds. I had the opportunity to work closely with the end users of this app, and gained invaluable experience incorporating their feedback.

Doc Sprout's Daycare

Role: Game designer and programmer, environmental artist.

In this project, I worked with a small team of students to create a quirky game about raising "doods" who grow "froot" out of their heads. We went from brainstorming ideas and barely usable code to fleshing out a design and a functioning prototype, all the while learning to incorporate player feedback and iterate on our game.

I contributed to the "dood" AI behavior tree. I programmed a simple lighting model and various visual effects, and also 3D modeled environmental assets. Throughout the project, I learned a lot about using Github effectively, and I gained experience both giving and recieving feedback on games.

Our original design was overly ambitious for an 8 week project, which lead to exhaustion and poor planning. We also had issues giving players proper feedback, leading to confusion and frustration. We ironed out many of our issues through frequent playtesting and constant iteration.

I learned about balancing mechanics with the overall vision for the project, and I grew as a programmer throughout the development process. The project had its challenges, but by working through them, I was able to become a better programmer and designer.

Ringo + Zingo

Role: Game designer and programmer, environmental artist | Play the game here

In this course, I worked with a group of students to design and develop a co-op 3D platformer in which 2 players jointly control a single character. After a few initial design lessons taught by a current professional and a brainstorming stage, we jumped into development and playtesting, during which we learned a lot about picking which mechanics did and did not contribute to our intended final design.

I programmed the character and camera movement, working hard to get them to feel natural, and I created many enviornmental hazards. I took part in animating various models and character actions. Through frequent playtesting sessions, I gained valuable experience in both critiquing and iterating games.

As a team, we faced conflicts of interest in level design, but through iteration, we managed to reach a design we could all be happy with. We also faced scheduling issues due to the late addition of several mechanics, but successfully finished the game through careful time management.

Through this course, I learned about the basics of game design, and gained firsthand experience designing and developing a game. I worked with a team to create an experience we all could be happy with, and learned a lot about project planning and cooperation.

GMTK 2018 Game Jam

Role: Game designer and programmer | Play the game here

A friend and I teamed up to participate in the 2018 Game Maker's Toolkit Game Jam, where over 1,000 participants were challenged to create a game in 48 hours in which one of the core mechanics of the game's genre was removed. We designed and created a fighting game in which the player cannot attack, instead requiring them to dodge attacks until the enemies become exhausted.

I programmed a tile based movement system with simple collision handling. I coded player and enemy behavior with animation driven hit checking and random variations in attack timing. I also created systems for playing sound effects and triggering UI behaviors, item collection/usage, and level changes.

We found it challenging to design a game in 48 hours that could quickly teach players and provide a fun gaming experience. In order to account for our deadline, we constrained the game to its core idea, leaving out anything that did not contribute to the core experience. We had to quickly teach player how to play our game, so we created a training dummy to safely teach players the core idea, and placed the first key in range of the dummy to require players to interact with it.

This project taught me about about designing for given constraints. We had a small timeframe and a tricky theme to incorporate, but it taught us a lot about the game design process. We had plenty of fun with this project, and learned about communication and simplicity in design along the way.
